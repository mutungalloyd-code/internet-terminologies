Deep in the hydrothermal vents of the ocean floor, life thrives in conditions that should be impossible. Here, giant tube worms—some reaching lengths of over two meters—survive without a digestive tract, eyes, or sunlight. Instead of eating, they host billions of symbiotic bacteria that convert the toxic hydrogen sulfide spewing from the Earth's crust into usable energy through a process called **chemosynthesis**. In this pitch-black environment, the water pressure is enough to crush a submarine, yet these resilient ecosystems represent a completely independent branch of life that doesn't rely on the sun, proving that "habitable" is a very relative term.
